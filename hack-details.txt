Yes — but only partially. Here's the precise picture:



So the direct answer is: yes, OAuth login solves the primary rate limit problem for any normal repo. A medium repo needs ~200 requests and OAuth gives you 5,000/hour — you're using 4% of your quota per scan. You'd have to be deliberately stress-testing to hit the hourly wall.

The thing that will actually bite you is the secondary rate limit — which has nothing to do with OAuth. GitHub watches your concurrency pattern, not just your total count. If you naively write Promise.all(prs.map(fetchDiff)) to fetch 50 PR diffs at once, you'll hit a 403 within seconds regardless of how you're authenticated. The fix is a concurrency limiter — a pool of max 5 simultaneous requests with a small delay between each. Libraries like p-limit (Node.js, one line of setup) handle this entirely.

The other concrete win from OAuth beyond rate limits: you get the user's GitHub identity for free, which means your contributor profiling feature knows who is doing the scanning — useful if you want to let people see their own repo's analysis tied to their account. And private repo access comes automatically once they authorize the repo scope, which removes an entire class of confusing 404 errors you'd otherwise have to debug.

The one thing OAuth genuinely cannot help with is very large repos — a monorepo with 10,000 commits and 500 open PRs will still take 10+ minutes to fully scan even at optimal concurrency. The solution there isn't auth, it's scope-limiting the scan (last 90 days, last 50 PRs) and showing progressive results as each module finishes, rather than waiting for everything before showing anything.

Yes — this is exactly the right architectural instinct. It's called on-demand lazy analysis and it's how production tools like SonarCloud and CodeClimate actually work. You just independently arrived at a senior engineering pattern.

Let me break down precisely how this changes everything:



The deeper reason this is the right call goes beyond rate limits — it's about user mental model. When someone pastes a repo URL, they don't know yet which PRs they care about or which contributor they're suspicious of. Forcing a 5-minute full scan before they see anything means they're staring at a loading spinner without knowing if the tool is even useful for their repo. With your event-driven approach, they see the overview in 30 seconds, get curious about a specific PR or contributor, click it, and the analysis appears in 10 seconds. That's a dramatically better product experience and it will show in your demo.

The one new problem worth thinking through carefully is the partial state UI. Your dashboard will simultaneously show items that are scored (user clicked them), items that are in-progress (spinning), and items that have only a surface-level tier-1 estimate. You need a clear visual language distinguishing these three states — otherwise the dashboard looks half-broken. A simple pattern: gray placeholder badge for "not yet analysed," a pulse animation for "analysing," and a colored score badge for "done." Users immediately understand the model without any explanation.

The client-side queue for concurrent clicks is also non-negotiable. Use p-limit with a concurrency of 2 — one line of setup, prevents you from racing into GitHub's secondary rate limit no matter how fast the user clicks.
